Accountability as the real design problem for small business AI. Why the hard part of agent infrastructure is not what the agent can do — it is who stays accountable for what it does.

Summary

Executive Summary

AI agents have become practical for small businesses. They can now read files, send messages, update records, and carry real operational work — not as a demo, but in live use, today.

That means capability is no longer the only issue. The harder problem is accountability: once an agent can act on its own, someone has to stay responsible for what it does. Who approved the action? What was it allowed to do? What was off-limits? What did it remember, what got logged, and how do you stop it when something goes wrong?

Small businesses need practical answers to those questions — not an enterprise governance program. They need memory rules, approval gates, tool boundaries, audit trails, stop controls, and a clean rollback path, built in a way an operator can actually use between rushes.

McPherson AI approaches this from the operator side. I am a working restaurant general manager, not an AI governance vendor. This paper lays out the control problem as it actually shows up in a small business, and the practical approach I have built around it.

Section 01

The Quiet Process

Sixteen years of running restaurants taught me where operations actually break, and it is almost never the moment everyone is watching.

The line cook slammed during a Friday rush is not the hidden risk. Everyone can see that. People step in.

The risk is the walk-in cooler that has been running three degrees too warm for two days because nobody checked — the quiet process running in the background that no one is accountable for until it becomes a problem you can no longer fix cheaply.

That is the lesson that translates directly to AI agents. The thing that runs unsupervised is the thing that hurts you.

Most of the conversation about agents right now is about capability. What can the agent do? How autonomous can it be? How many tasks can it complete end to end? Those are real questions, and the answers are getting more impressive every month. Earlier this month, the New York Times Magazine profiled small business owners already running real operations — finances, customer service, email, bookings — on AI agents with minimal supervision. A bankruptcy lawyer running his casework on them. A pilot running his side business on seven of them. A landlord who let his assistant go because the agents handle the tickets now.

The capability is here. That is no longer the interesting part.

The same article is just as clear about what goes wrong. One user — an alignment director, whose actual job is making AI behave — discovered her agent deleting important emails and, in her words, had to run to her computer to stop it because she could not stop it from her phone. Security researchers quoted in the piece call the underlying prompt-injection problem largely unsolved. Agents that piloted themselves well for weeks suddenly drove into a wall.

The interesting part — the part that decides whether any of this works inside a real business — is what happens when the agent acts. Who is accountable for it. Whether anyone is watching the quiet process at all.

Section 02

Capability Is the Easy Part

In a restaurant, a warehouse, or any operating business, every action has an owner.

The labor decision belongs to the GM. The vendor escalation belongs to the operator. The food-safety call belongs to whoever is on the floor. Authority is structural, not optional. You cannot run a store where nobody owns the decisions, and you cannot run one where everybody owns all of them. The structure is the thing that makes the business function.

An agent that takes action without preserving that structure does not reduce the operator’s load. It creates a new kind of uncertainty: who is responsible when the agent gets it wrong?

This is the question that almost no one is designing for, and it is the question every operator asks the moment the demo ends. An agent can look flawless in a controlled example. A real deployment asks harder things:

  • Which actions can the agent take on its own?
  • Which require a human to confirm first?
  • Which are off-limits regardless of how confident the agent is?
  • Who reviews what the agent actually did?
  • How does the operator override or correct it?

None of those are capability questions. They are accountability questions. And in a small business — where there is no IT department, no procurement team, no AI governance committee — they are the questions that decide whether the operator trusts the system enough to let it run at all.

Capability is the easier part. Bounded action, role-aware authority, and a clear human in the loop are what determine whether an operator actually trusts the system. For small businesses especially, the agent’s job is not to be more autonomous than the operator. It is to make the operator’s authority more leveraged.

Section 03

The Rule Has to Exist Before the Action

Here is the principle the whole problem reduces to:

An agent should not decide its own authority at runtime.

The rules for what it can do automatically, what needs confirmation, what needs to escalate, and what is simply forbidden have to exist before the action — not be worked out in the moment by the agent itself, and not discovered by the business after something has already gone wrong.

That last part is where almost everyone ends up. When you talk to people actually running agents in production, a pattern shows up again and again: teams discover their list of dangerous actions reactively — after a wrong message went out under someone’s name, after a record got changed that another person acted on, after a write happened that could not be cleanly undone. The boundary gets drawn the day after it was needed.

The whole proposition of governed agent infrastructure is to move that line earlier. To define authority upfront, on purpose, while it is still cheap — instead of after it has broken trust.

Section 04

Three Buckets, and the Drift Nobody Watches

The most useful way I have found to make this concrete — and the one that operators actually understand — is to sort every action an agent might take into three buckets.

Auto. Low stakes, easily reversible, nobody needs to look. Reading a shift note. Summarizing an approved document. Building an internal follow-up list. Flagging a pattern for a human to review. The agent can do these on its own because the cost of getting one wrong is small and recoverable.

Confirm. Moderate stakes. The agent prepares the action but a human approves before it happens. This is where most of the real value and most of the real risk live.

Forbidden. Never automated, regardless of how confident the agent is. Sending payments. Deleting records. Changing payroll. Anything where “the agent was pretty sure” is not an acceptable answer if it goes wrong.

Low-stakes, reversible actions the agent can do alone.

Examples: read shift notes; summarize approved documents; build internal follow-up lists; flag patterns for review.

The agent prepares the action; a human approves before it executes.

Examples: send outbound messages; change schedules; approve refunds; edit records others will act on; place orders.

ForbiddenNever automated

Never automated, regardless of confidence.

Examples: send payments; delete records; change payroll; modify secrets; make legal, HR, or financial decisions.

What goes in the middle bucket is the heart of it. When I talk to operators and other people building real agent workflows, the line they keep drawing is the same one: a human has to confirm anything that changes the business state or represents the operator externally. Sending a message under your name. Changing a schedule. Approving a refund. Editing a record someone else will act on. Placing an order. The agent can draft, summarize, organize, and flag all day. But the moment an action goes out into the world as if the operator did it, or changes something that is hard to undo, a person should be in the loop.

This is not a hard concept. Operators get it instantly, because it is how they already run their stores. The GM does not let a new hire commit the Thursday order without a second look. The confirm bucket is just that instinct, applied to a system instead of a person.

There is one part of this that almost everyone misses, though, and it is the part that ties straight back to the walk-in cooler.

The buckets drift.

As an operator’s trust builds, they naturally want to move actions out of confirm and into auto. That is healthy — it is the system earning its keep. But if that drift is silent, something dangerous happens: the system quietly becomes more autonomous than anyone ever actually approved. Action by action, confirmation by confirmation, the boundary moves, and no one decided to move it. One day you look up and the agent is running large parts of the business on permissions that were never formally granted — they just eroded into place.

That is the cooler running warm. Not a dramatic failure. A quiet drift in a process nobody was watching, until it costs you.

There is a second, quieter version of this drift, and it is the more dangerous one — because nobody ever decides to move the boundary at all.

Confirmation is not a stable state by default. At the start, confirm means a human actually reviews. But as trust builds and the queue grows, the human gets faster. Approvals become muscle memory. The system still says a human is in the loop — the button still says confirm — but in practice the person has stopped reviewing and started rubber-stamping. The control still exists on paper while everyone has quietly trained themselves out of using it. Nobody moved the action from confirm to auto. It just became auto, with a human pressing the button out of habit.

The dangerous part is not that the agent runs alone. It is believing a human is reviewing when they have effectively stopped. And this tends to spike at exactly the wrong time — when the operator is slammed, which is also when a bad action is most likely to slip through.

The saving grace is that this drift is not invisible. It leaves a trail. The shape of how a human approves changes in ways that can be watched — which means drift can be caught before it costs you, not discovered after.

So the drift has to be explicit. Logged. Reviewable. When an action moves from confirm to auto, that should be a decision someone made on purpose and can see in a record — not an accident of accumulated convenience. A governed system does not just set boundaries once and walk away. It watches its own boundaries move.

Section 05

What “Defining It Upfront” Actually Looks Like

For a small business, governance cannot mean an admin panel with two hundred toggles and a policy document nobody reads. The operator does not have time for that, and a system that demands it will simply be ignored in the middle of a rush.

Practical governance for a small business is a short, honest conversation, captured as rules, before the agent ever touches a live workflow. It answers a handful of questions in the operator’s own words:

  • Where does follow-up get lost today?
  • What does the manager keep in their head that the system should carry instead?
  • What gets forgotten after the rush?
  • What recurring issue shows up every week?
  • And the most important one: what should never be automated, no matter how good the agent gets?

That last question is the one most technology skips, and it is the one that earns trust. When an operator hears you ask what the system should never do, the whole relationship changes. You are not there to replace their judgment. You are there to carry the part of the job that keeps slipping through the cracks — and to leave the part that has to stay theirs, theirs.

Out of that conversation comes the actual governance:

A clear map of which actions are auto, which need confirmation, and which are forbidden.

A human approval gate sitting in front of anything irreversible, external, or consequential.

A memory policy — not “remember everything,” but remember what is unresolved, repeating, costly to miss, and tied to this store. Structured operational context, not a chat transcript.

An audit trail, so that when something happens, the operator can reconstruct what the agent did and why.

A way to stop. An off switch that pauses the system cleanly when something feels wrong, no engineer required.

None of this is exotic. It is the difference between an AI demo and a system a real business can lean on.

Section 06

What I Built to Prove It

I did not want this to stay a thesis, so I built a working version of it.

Before putting anything in front of a live store, I assembled and verified a private governed pilot base — a controlled environment that proves the approach is real, not just an argument. At the outcome level, it includes:

A governance layer that classifies actions and enforces approval gates, with the operator as the human who approves anything consequential.

A structured memory layer built for operational context — store notes, handoffs, follow-ups, recurring issues — rather than raw conversation history.

An audit trail, so agent behavior can be reviewed after the fact.

Secrets and environment discipline, so sensitive credentials and customer data never leak into a reusable system.

A deployment and recovery process with a clean, known-good base and a documented rollback path.

A human approval gate at every step that matters: nothing goes live, reactivates, or touches a real workflow without a person signing off.

The internal architecture stays private. What matters here is the outcome: a verified, secret-free, recoverable foundation with a human in the loop by design — assembled, tested, and approved before a single live deployment.

Section 07

Why Small Business Is the Right Proving Ground

It would be easy to assume governance is an enterprise problem — that the small end of the market is too simple to need it. It is the opposite.

Small businesses are the place where the risks are real but the workflows are concrete. A bad automated action in a restaurant is not an abstraction: it is a wrong ticket in the kitchen, a refund that should not have gone out, a schedule change nobody approved, a message sent to a customer under the owner’s name. The consequences are immediate and they land on a person who cannot absorb them the way a large company can.

And small businesses have none of the protective layers enterprises take for granted. No IT department to catch the mistake. No procurement process to vet the tool. No governance committee to define the boundaries. The owner is all of those things, usually while also working the floor.

That means the governance cannot be heavy, and it cannot be optional. It has to be simple enough to set up in a conversation, clear enough to trust, and bounded enough that the operator never feels they have handed over control.

If you can solve governance here — in the hardest, leanest, least-forgiving environment — the approach travels. The dental practice, the plumbing company, the small agency all have the same shape of problem. The restaurant is just the sharpest version of it, which is exactly why it is the right place to prove the answer.

Section 08

The Bar

The conversation about agents will keep racing toward capability, because capability demos well and sells the future. But the businesses actually running these systems are already learning the harder lesson, the one every operator knows in their bones: the danger is the quiet process nobody owns.

So the bar is not “smart.” Smart is table stakes now, and it is getting cheaper every month.

The bar is contained, observable, and pausable.

Before you trust an agent with anything that can cost you money, data, or customers: an agent that cannot be bounded, watched, and stopped is not an asset. It is a walk-in cooler running warm in the back, and you will not know what it cost you until you open the door.

That is the problem I work on at McPherson AI: making agents useful inside a real business without making the operator give up the one thing that actually runs the place — their judgment about what should never happen without them.

If you are experimenting with agents in a real business, I would be interested in comparing notes. What is working, what scared you, and where you think the guardrails should go.
